# BioProver: AI-Guided Formal Verification and Parameter Repair for Synthetic Biology Circuits

## Problem and Approach

Synthetic biology has a fatal quality-assurance gap. Engineers design gene circuits—toggle switches, oscillators, logic gates, biosensors—by hand-tuning ODE models and running Monte Carlo simulations, then hoping that wet-lab behavior matches in silico predictions. When circuits fail (at >60% rates in first build-test cycles), the feedback loop costs months and thousands of dollars per iteration. The field has no equivalent of a type-checker: no formal guarantee that a design meets its temporal specification before DNA is ordered. Cello, the state of the art in genetic circuit design automation, demonstrated 45/60 circuits working as predicted—but provides *statistical* predictions, not *formal* guarantees, and offers no actionable guidance when designs fail. PRISM and Storm handle probabilistic model checking but cannot scale to the nonlinear ODEs of real gene circuits. dReal solves nonlinear arithmetic but has no temporal-logic model checking pipeline, no biology-specific abstractions, and no synthesis capability. iBioSim provides CAD for genetic circuits but lacks SMT-based reasoning and systematic parameter uncertainty handling. No existing tool answers the question a computational bioengineer actually needs answered: *"Will my circuit work under realistic parameter uncertainty—and if not, how exactly should I fix it?"*

BioProver closes this gap with a verify-diagnose-repair pipeline grounded in three mature but previously disconnected theories: δ-decidability for bounded model checking of nonlinear ODEs (Gao et al.), finite-state abstraction of stochastic biochemical kinetics via moment closure (Engblom, Munsky), and counterexample-guided abstraction refinement with learned heuristics (Clarke, Grumberg, Jha). The critical insight is that this integration is *not just plumbing*—it creates emergent algorithmic challenges absent from any individual tool:

1. **Hybrid abstract domain.** The CEGAR loop must refine over a product of interval abstractions (continuous ODE state), discrete stochastic modes (low-copy-number gene expression), and universally quantified parameters (uncertainty envelopes). Standard CEGAR operates over finite-state abstractions with a single refinement dimension; BioProver's abstract domain is a three-factor product requiring coordinated refinement across fundamentally different mathematical structures.

2. **Nested quantifier alternation.** Parameter repair embeds a CEGIS loop *inside* each CEGAR iteration, creating ∃∀ quantifier alternation: find parameter values such that *for all* uncertainty instantiations, the specification holds. Each CEGIS iteration invokes the full CEGAR verifier. Convergence of this nested structure requires novel analysis showing that each counterexample eliminates a measurable region of the parameter×uncertainty product space.

3. **Biology-specific refinement operators with provable acceleration.** Gene regulatory networks exhibit pervasive monotonicity (~70% of interactions in *E. coli* are monotone). BioProver exploits this structure through refinement operators—Hill-threshold predicates, monotonicity-guided vertex-only checking, time-scale-guided temporal splitting—that achieve provable convergence acceleration for monotone GRN subsystems, reducing refinement from exponential to logarithmic in precision for monotone dimensions.

The user writes a temporal-logic specification over observable phenotypes using Bio-STL, a domain-specific front-end providing biologist-accessible templates for common behaviors (oscillation, bistability, adaptation, pulse generation). BioProver encodes the parameterized ODE/CME hybrid model as a quantified SMT problem over real arithmetic, partitioning the model into a decidable polynomial fragment (mass-action, integer-Hill kinetics via Tarski's theorem) and a δ-decidable transcendental fragment (non-integer Hill, exponential degradation via dReal). The CEGAR engine iteratively refines abstract state-space partitions using biology-informed predicate templates derived from Hill-function thresholds, nullcline structure, and Jacobian eigenspaces—with a lightweight neural network trained on prior verification traces to predict which refinement predicates will eliminate spurious counterexamples fastest. When verification fails, BioProver extracts a constructive counterexample and synthesizes a *minimal parameter repair*: the smallest perturbation to promoter strengths, RBS efficiencies, or degradation tags that would make the specification satisfiable across the full uncertainty envelope—giving the biologist an actionable redesign, not just a red light.

Bounded-guarantee verification is BioProver's primary output mode for realistic circuits. Rather than binary proved/disproved, BioProver reports: (a) fraction of parameter uncertainty volume verified, (b) geometric characterization of the unverified region, (c) whether the unverified region corresponds to biologically plausible parameter combinations. Full formal verification is available for circuits within the compositional feasibility frontier (8 or fewer species after decomposition).

The lead technical novelty is **AI-guided CEGAR for biology**: the specific triple intersection of AI-guided abstraction refinement, formal verification of nonlinear hybrid systems, and gene regulatory network dynamics is genuinely unexplored. Prior work has applied AI-guided CEGAR to neural network verification (NARv, Marabou) and real-time systems, but biological systems pose fundamentally different abstraction challenges: the state space is continuous and high-dimensional, dynamics are governed by nonlinear Hill-function kinetics with transcendental terms, parameter uncertainty spans orders of magnitude, and hybrid stochastic-deterministic semantics arise from low-copy-number gene expression. This is not "dReal + PRISM + Cello reimplemented with a thin ML layer."

BioProver proves properties of a *model*, not of a *cell*—and this distinction is stated explicitly. Formal guarantees are conditioned on the fidelity of the ODE/CME model and the accuracy of parameter ranges drawn from published characterization data. What BioProver eliminates is *design error*: failures attributable to the circuit topology and parameter choices, which are distinct from (and additive with) model-parameter uncertainty. By separating these two sources of failure, BioProver enables a rational design workflow: verify the design against the model, then validate the model against the organism. This is precisely the workflow that semiconductor design-rule checking enabled for VLSI—and synthetic biology is at the same inflection point today.

The scalability wall—formal verification of systems with more than ~10 species—is a real limitation, but 3–8 species circuits are exactly where formal verification has highest leverage. These are precisely the circuits that automated design tools like Cello produce: algorithmically designed from characterized part libraries. Formal verification of algorithmically-designed circuits is the natural analogue of verification in hardware synthesis, where design-rule checking targets the output of place-and-route tools, not hand-drawn schematics. The circuits Cello designs are small enough to verify and important enough to get right.

BioProver is positioned as complementary to the existing genetic design automation ecosystem, not competitive. It imports SBML and SBOL models, integrates with Cello's characterized gate libraries via a dedicated import pathway, and exports verified designs in standard formats. The Cello re-verification study—formally checking which of Cello's published 60 circuits are robustly correct under parameter uncertainty—is the headline result that demonstrates three specific failure modes BioProver catches: (1) *fragile circuits* that satisfy specifications at nominal parameter values but fail under realistic uncertainty envelopes, (2) *repairable failures* where Cello's optimizer selected parameters that miss a nearby verified region BioProver can find, and (3) *robustness gradients* ranking circuits by quantitative distance-from-failure, enabling engineers to select the most robust design among functionally equivalent alternatives. Engagement with the iBioSim community (Chris Myers' group, CU Boulder) ensures complementary positioning with the closest existing design tool.

## Value Proposition

**Who desperately needs this:** Computational bioengineers at synbio companies (Ginkgo, Asimov, Octant Bio) and advanced academic labs who already build ODE models of their circuits—researchers who currently simulate but cannot formally verify. Most critically, the cell therapy industry building genetic safety switches for CAR-T and engineered probiotics, where the difference between "probably correct" and "provably correct" determines whether a design proceeds to expensive GLP studies. BioProver's primary users are computational biologists who are comfortable with ODE models and want formal guarantees before committing to wet-lab builds.

**What becomes possible:** For the first time, a computational bioengineer can ask "will my circuit work under realistic uncertainty?" and receive a machine-checked answer—and if the answer is no, the tool tells them exactly how to fix it. Specifically: (1) *Formal correctness certificates* for genetic circuit designs, with bounded-guarantee coverage reports for circuits beyond the full-verification frontier; (2) *Quantitative robustness margins* via STL robustness semantics, telling engineers not just whether a circuit works but *how far from failure* it operates; (3) *Automated parameter repair* that converts every counterexample into an actionable redesign suggestion with formal guarantees; (4) *Robustness ranking* across design alternatives, enabling selection of the most robust circuit from a library of functionally equivalent options.

**Honest regulatory value:** BioProver does not produce documents FDA currently accepts. The real regulatory value is pre-submission risk reduction: catching design-level failures before expensive GLP studies. For cell therapy programs where a failed GLP study costs $500K–$2M, eliminating design errors computationally has clear ROI. A single caught design error saves an estimated $2K–$5K in direct iteration costs and weeks of engineering time, with much larger downstream savings when the error would have propagated to GLP studies.

**Target community resonance:** The work bridges four areas of expertise represented by the target community—computational bioengineering and formal methods (Biddle), cell modeling and systems biology (Eder), AI for physical systems and formal methods (Y. Chen), and biological data modeling with AI integration (J. Chen). The AI-guided CEGAR component bridges formal methods (Biddle/Eder) with AI methodology (Chen/Chen) at a genuinely unexplored intersection. The hybrid stochastic/ODE model checking addresses a real modeling challenge that systems biologists face daily.

### The 5 Hardest Engineering Subproblems

**Three genuinely novel problems:**

1. **CEGAR convergence for hybrid stochastic systems (Sub 7, 6, 4).** Standard CEGAR was designed for finite-state software. Applying it to continuous state spaces with nonlinear dynamics, stochastic behavior, and interval-valued parameters requires fundamentally new refinement operators. The CEGAR loop must refine over a product of interval abstractions, discrete stochastic modes, and universally quantified parameters—three dimensions of abstraction that interact: refining the continuous abstraction changes which stochastic modes are reachable, which changes which parameter regions matter. Standard convergence results do not apply to this product domain. The interaction of continuous abstraction, stochastic counterexamples (probability distributions over paths, not single paths), and universal parameter quantification creates a fundamentally new convergence problem addressed by Theorem 4.

2. **Nested CEGIS-inside-CEGAR for parameter repair (Sub 9, 7).** Parameter repair is a ∃∀ problem: find parameter values such that *for all* uncertainty instantiations, the specification holds. Each CEGIS iteration invokes the full CEGAR verifier, creating a nested doubly-hard loop. Convergence requires that each counterexample eliminates a measurable region of the parameter×uncertainty product space. The novel convergence analysis (Theorem 5) shows that under δ-decidability, the nested loop produces ε-approximate solutions, and monotonicity in ≥70% of parameter dimensions reduces the effective search dimension from d to d_nonmonotone ≤ 10.

3. **Interpolant extraction from δ-satisfiability proofs (Sub 5).** Craig interpolation for nonlinear real arithmetic is an open problem. We extract interpolants from dReal's δ-proofs via proof post-processing—a novel technique that constrains the proof format and requires careful handling of the δ-relaxation to ensure interpolants are valid for the original (non-relaxed) problem. When interpolant extraction fails, CEGAR falls back to heuristic refinement with biology-informed predicate templates. In fallback mode, soundness is preserved (the verifier never produces false proofs), but completeness is lost (the verifier may fail to verify correct circuits). Bounded-guarantee mode continues to work because it reports verified coverage rather than requiring a complete proof.

**Two hard-but-solved problems requiring careful integration:**

4. **Sound nonlinear reachability via CAPD bridge (Sub 2, 5, 6).** Computing guaranteed ODE trajectory enclosures with Hill-function kinetics while fighting the wrapping effect. CAPD provides rigorous Taylor-model flowpipe computation, but the proof certificate bridge—translating CAPD's enclosures into δ-satisfiability proof fragments consumable by the SMT interpolation layer—is novel engineering. Biology-specific extensions exploit Hill-function monotonicity and GRN Jacobian sparsity for tighter enclosures.

5. **CPU-budget AI integration via ONNX Runtime (Sub 8).** ML heuristics must run in the inner loop of CEGAR and SMT solving where millisecond overhead compounds. All inference uses ONNX Runtime with int8 quantized weights, ≤100K parameters, and approximately 1ms inference per decision. The GNN circuit encoder handles variable-size graphs via sparse adjacency. Online learning updates from CEGAR traces without disrupting solver performance. This is solved technology (ONNX Runtime is mature), but careful integration is required to avoid latency penalties in the verification hot path.

## New Mathematics Required

Two core theoretical contributions and three supporting soundness results. Together, these five theorems directly enable the artifact—each is necessary, and removing any one prevents the system from producing its claimed output.

**Core contributions:**

**Theorem 4 (CEGAR Progress with Biology-Specific Operators and Monotonicity Acceleration, T4.2).** Each application of biology-aware refinement (Algorithm 4.1—structural refinement via Hill thresholds, monotonicity-guided refinement via input-output characteristics, time-scale-guided refinement via temporal splitting) eliminates at least one spurious counterexample and does not introduce new spurious counterexamples of shorter length. Combined with the bounded state space [0, x_max]^m and finite predicate lattice, CEGAR terminates in at most O(2^|P_max|) iterations, where |P_max| = O(m·r + m²) for m species and r reactions. For monotone GRN subsystems (~70% of interactions), convergence accelerates to O(m · log(x_max/ε)) via binary-search refinement on each species independently. *Why load-bearing:* Gates CEGAR termination. Without a progress guarantee, the verification loop may oscillate indefinitely, and the tool provides no answer. The monotonicity acceleration is the key theoretical contribution: it transforms CEGAR from exponential to near-linear for the dominant class of biological circuits. *Difficulty:* Medium-High—standard CEGAR termination adapted to the hybrid predicate-interval-stochastic product domain with novel biology-specific refinement operators and monotonicity acceleration proof.

**Theorem 5 (CEGIS Convergence under δ-Decidability with Dimension Reduction, T5.1).** For compact uncertainty set P_U and continuous dynamics M (w.r.t. parameters), the CEGIS parameter-repair loop converges in finitely many iterations if the verifier is exact, or produces an ε-approximate solution (robust within ε of the satisfiability boundary) if the verifier uses δ-decidability. Convergence requires at most O((diam(P_U)/ε)^d) iterations where d = dim(P_U). For the target scalability point (d = 30 uncertain parameters, ε-partitioning), the bound is infeasible naively—but monotonicity in ≥70% of parameter dimensions reduces the effective d to d_nonmonotone ≤ 10, and the GNN-guided synthesis surrogate (Subsystem 8) prunes the effective search by predicting which parameter subregions are most likely to satisfy the specification. *Why load-bearing:* Gates parameter repair termination. Without convergence analysis, the repair feature has no formal guarantee of progress, and the tool cannot promise actionable redesign suggestions. The ε-approximation analysis under δ-decidability and the monotonicity-based dimension reduction argument are the novel contributions. *Difficulty:* Medium-High—follows from compactness and continuity; the interaction between δ-relaxation and convergence guarantees, and the formal monotonicity dimension reduction, are technically subtle.

**Supporting soundness results:**

**Theorem 1 (Fragment Decomposition Correctness, T1.3).** Given a GRN model M with m species and r reactions, partition the kinetic terms into fragments: F₁ (polynomial NRA, decidable via Tarski) for mass-action and integer-Hill kinetics, F₂ (δ-NRA, δ-decidable via dReal) for non-integer Hill and transcendental terms, and F₃ (interval Taylor model over-approximation) for any undecidable remainder. The composite SMT encoding produces satisfying assignments that are sound over-approximations of M's reachable states. *Why load-bearing:* Gates the entire SMT encoding pipeline. Without compositional soundness across heterogeneous theory fragments, the verifier cannot handle real biological models that mix polynomial and transcendental kinetics. *Difficulty:* Medium-High—requires proving that the interface conditions between fragments preserve the simulation relation under δ-relaxation.

**Theorem 2 (Adaptive Gridding Soundness, T1.8).** The finite-state abstraction produced by biologically-informed adaptive gridding—combining time-scale separation (Fenichel's theorem applied to fast species), monotonicity exploitation (vertex-only evaluation for monotone subsystems, reducing cost from 2^m to 2^m_nonmonotone), and gene-copy symmetry reduction—is a sound over-approximation of concrete GRN dynamics. Formally, there exists a simulation relation R ⊆ S_abstract × S_concrete such that every concrete behavior is represented by an abstract behavior. *Why load-bearing:* Gates state-space abstraction for model checking. Without this theorem, the CEGAR loop operates on an abstraction with no formal relationship to the concrete system. *Difficulty:* Medium-High—each reduction (time-scale, monotonicity, symmetry) is individually known; proving the combined simulation relation is preserved under all three simultaneously is novel.

**Theorem 3 (Robustness-Aware SMT Encoding, T2.4).** The STL robustness degree ρ(φ, **x**, t)—the quantitative distance to satisfaction/violation—can be encoded in SMT by replacing Boolean satisfaction with real-valued constraints and min/max with auxiliary variables, with encoding size linear in formula size and polynomial in time steps. For interval-valued trajectories, robustness bounds propagate soundly: ρ⁻ > 0 implies φ is robustly satisfied for all trajectories in the enclosure. For parameter repair, the synthesis objective becomes: maximize ρ subject to ∃**p**_D ∈ P_D: ρ(φ, **x**(**p**_D, **p**_U)) ≥ ρ_min for all **p**_U ∈ P_U. *Why load-bearing:* Gates both verification quality (robustness margins) and parameter repair (robustness maximization as synthesis objective). Without this encoding, repair degenerates to binary feasibility search with no quantitative guidance. *Difficulty:* Medium—the encoding itself is novel; careful handling of non-monotonicity of ρ w.r.t. interval state variables is the technical challenge.

## Best Paper Argument

BioProver merits best-paper consideration at CAV (tool track) for five reasons:

1. **Headline result: Cello re-verification.** The Cello re-verification study—formally checking which of Cello's 60 published circuits are robustly correct under parameter uncertainty—is a compelling, concrete result. It answers a question the synbio community has wondered about since 2016: *which* of Cello's 45 "working" circuits are robustly correct, which are fragile, and which can be repaired? This single study demonstrates all three of BioProver's novel capabilities (verification, robustness quantification, repair) on the field's most-cited benchmark.

2. **Genuinely novel algorithmic contribution.** The emergent challenges from integrating CEGAR with nonlinear hybrid biological systems are not "just integration." The three core novelties—(a) CEGAR over a product of interval, stochastic, and parameter abstractions, (b) nested CEGIS-inside-CEGAR with novel convergence analysis under δ-decidability, (c) biology-specific refinement operators with provable monotonicity acceleration—are contributions to formal methods that generalize beyond biology. The two core theorems (T4: CEGAR progress with monotonicity acceleration; T5: CEGIS convergence under δ-decidability with dimension reduction) are supported by three soundness results (T1: fragment decomposition, T2: adaptive gridding, T3: robustness encoding).

3. **Compelling evaluation with baselines.** Comparing against PRISM (discrete-state only), dReal (no temporal logic), Breach (falsification only, no formal guarantees), and iBioSim (no SMT-based reasoning) on a curated benchmark suite of 10+ circuits demonstrates clear capability gaps. The Cello study includes parameter-range sensitivity analysis over choice of uncertainty distributions, demonstrating robustness of results to modeling assumptions. All evaluation is fully automated with no human annotation.

4. **High practical stakes with honest framing.** Formal verification of algorithmically-designed circuits—exactly the circuits Cello and similar tools produce—is the natural analogue of design-rule checking in hardware synthesis. BioProver catches design-level failures before wet-lab builds, with clear ROI for cell therapy programs where failed GLP studies cost $500K–$2M. The tool does not overclaim regulatory acceptance; it provides pre-submission risk reduction.

5. **Cross-community impact.** For formal methods (CAV tool track): δ-complete reasoning scales to a high-impact real-world domain beyond cyber-physical systems. For synthetic biology (ACS Synthetic Biology, Nature Biotech): faith-based simulation is replaced by bounded guarantees with quantitative coverage. For AI/ML: learned heuristics accelerate sound formal reasoning without sacrificing soundness—a template applicable to any CEGAR-based verification system.

## Evaluation Plan

All evaluation is fully automated with zero human involvement. Metrics, baselines, and benchmarks:

### Benchmark Suite
- **Curated circuits (10+):** Toggle switch (Gardner et al.), repressilator (Elowitz & Leibler), feed-forward loops (all 8 types), band-pass filter, genetic NAND/NOR gates, cascade circuits of depth 2–5, the Cello library of 60 published circuits. Each circuit has published parameter values with measured uncertainty ranges.
- **Scalable generators:** Parameterized toggle switch with n stages (n = 1..5), repressilator with n nodes (n = 3..8), cascade of depth d (d = 2..10). Properties generated automatically from circuit structure.

### Metrics (all automated)
1. **Verification rate:** Fraction of circuits verified within the time budget, by circuit complexity (species count, parameter count).
2. **Wall-clock time:** Seconds to verify/falsify each benchmark, measured by automated harness.
3. **CEGAR iterations:** Number of refinement iterations to convergence, measuring AI-guided acceleration vs. baseline heuristics.
4. **Robustness margin:** Quantitative STL robustness of verified circuits (higher = more robust design).
5. **Repair success rate:** Fraction of falsified circuits for which parameter repair produces a verified fix.
6. **Repair perturbation magnitude:** L2 norm of parameter change required for repair (smaller = more practical).
7. **Bounded-guarantee coverage:** Percentage of uncertainty volume verified in bounded-guarantee mode, reported for all circuits including those where full verification succeeds (to characterize the coverage-time tradeoff).

### Baseline Comparisons (all automated, scripted interfaces)
- **PRISM/Storm:** Export circuit to CTMC, model-check equivalent properties. Demonstrates limitations of discrete-state abstraction for nonlinear ODE dynamics.
- **dReal (standalone):** Encode the same verification query as raw NRA. Demonstrates value of CEGAR + biology-specific abstractions + temporal logic pipeline.
- **Breach:** Run STL falsification on same circuits. Demonstrates gap between falsification (can find bugs) and verification (can prove absence of bugs).
- **iBioSim:** Compare model-checking results on shared benchmarks. Demonstrates value of SMT-based reasoning and parameter uncertainty handling.
- **BioProver (no AI):** Ablation study removing the ML heuristic engine, using standard CEGAR heuristics. Measures the contribution of AI-guided refinement.

### Cello Re-Verification Study
Import all 60 circuits from Cello's published library (Nielsen et al., Science 2016). For each circuit: (1) encode using Cello's characterized gate transfer functions with published uncertainty ranges, (2) specify the intended Boolean logic function as a Bio-STL property, (3) run BioProver verification, (4) classify as *robustly correct* (verified), *fragile* (verified only for reduced uncertainty), *repairable* (counterexample found but parameter repair succeeds), or *incorrect/timeout*. Compare against Cello's experimental success rate (45/60). This study is fully automated—circuit models and properties are derived programmatically from Cello's published data. Additionally, perform parameter-range sensitivity analysis: repeat the study under three different assumptions about parameter uncertainty distributions (uniform, log-normal, and truncated Gaussian) to assess robustness of the classification to distributional modeling choices.

## Laptop CPU Feasibility

BioProver runs entirely on a modern laptop CPU (e.g., Apple M2 or Intel i7, 8 cores, 16 GB RAM). No GPU is required at any stage. Concrete performance analysis:

**Why this is feasible:**

1. **SMT solving is CPU-bound, not GPU-bound.** dReal and Z3 are CPU-native. The interval constraint propagation approach (branch-and-prune with contractors) is inherently sequential with limited parallelism—SIMD helps for batched interval operations, but GPU dispatch overhead would dominate. Our portfolio strategy runs dReal and Z3 in parallel across CPU cores.

2. **ML inference is small.** All neural models have ≤100K parameters. GNN inference on a circuit graph with ≤50 nodes takes <10ms on CPU via ONNX Runtime. The predicate prediction MLP uses int8 quantized weights with approximately 1ms inference per decision via ONNX Runtime on CPU. No training happens during verification; models are pre-trained on the circuit library.

3. **CAPD-backed validated ODE integration.** CAPD's rigorous interval ODE integration is CPU-native and cache-friendly. Taylor model coefficients for a 10-species system fit in L2 cache. The proof certificate bridge adds overhead for certificate production but does not change the core integration performance characteristics.

4. **Compositional verification is the scaling strategy.** A 10-species circuit is decomposed into 2–3 modules of 3–5 species each. Each module is verified independently (parallelizable across cores), then composed via assume-guarantee contracts. This reduces the effective verification complexity from exponential in 10 to exponential in 5.

5. **Three algorithmic shortcuts for CPU feasibility.** (a) *Monotonicity exploitation:* for a 10-species system with 7 monotone interactions, vertex-only checking reduces the parameter-space evaluation from 2^10 = 1024 to 2^3 = 8 corners—a 128× speedup. (b) *Incremental SMT solving:* CEGAR iterations share most of their constraint structure; push/pop and assumption-based reasoning avoid re-encoding, providing 3–10× speedup over monolithic solving. (c) *Simulation-guided abstraction:* fast ODE simulation identifies regions of the state space that are dynamically unreachable, eliminating 50–80% of CEGAR iterations by starting with a coarse abstraction that only refines where simulations indicate potential counterexamples.

6. **Moment-based abstraction accelerates stochastic analysis.** Instead of running FSP on the full CME (exponential in species count), moment closure provides conservative bounds on low-order moments, sufficient for most Bio-STL properties. This is used as a *performance optimization*, not a formal abstraction—when tighter bounds are needed, FSP is invoked on the reduced (compositionally decomposed) module.

**Concrete performance targets:**

| Circuit Complexity | Species | Parameters | Verification Mode | Target Wall-Clock |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Simple (toggle switch) | 3 | 8 | Full verification | <2 minutes |
| Medium (repressilator) | 5 | 12 | Full verification | <15 minutes |
| Complex (cascade + feedback) | 8 | 20 | Compositional verification | <45 minutes |

**Bounded-guarantee as primary output mode:** Bounded-guarantee verification is BioProver's primary output mode for realistic circuits. Rather than binary proved/disproved, BioProver reports: (a) fraction of parameter uncertainty volume verified, (b) geometric characterization of the unverified region (convex hull in parameter space), (c) whether the unverified region corresponds to biologically plausible parameter combinations (cross-referenced against literature parameter ranges). Full formal verification is available for circuits within the compositional feasibility frontier (8 or fewer species after decomposition). For circuits beyond this frontier, bounded-guarantee mode provides useful quantitative information: e.g., "specification verified for 73% of the uncertainty volume, with the unverified region concentrated in high-K_d, low-n parameter combinations that are rare in characterized part libraries."

Properties are generated from templates or specified in Bio-STL files. Baselines are invoked via command-line wrappers. Results are collected, analyzed, and tabulated by the evaluation harness. Plots are generated programmatically. No human annotation, labeling, user studies, or subjective evaluation at any stage.
